# BotC Skill Axes — Core Definitions

## 1. Logic
The ability to make rational conclusions based purely on the information available. Great logical players can solve the game single-handedly for the good team, and can make stronger decisions as an evil player that make the game increasingly challenging.

New players begin at vastly different levels of competency. Logic is a slow skill to grow if a player isn't already logically inclined. Some players never quite become "the solver" — preferring to lean on the more social axes.

## 2. Game Knowledge
Understanding of the rules and mechanics of BotC. Veterans can solve storyteller puzzles simply by having seen them before (e.g. when the Recluse misregisters for the Investigator).

Key insight: **Game Knowledge is a multiplicative boost to all other axes.** It's easier to form logic lines, form convincing lies, persuade the town, and pick up tells when you're ultra familiar with the game. It separates a veteran from a "smart" new player.

Serves double-duty on evil: knowing patterns of play allows faster, more convincing disinformation. New players with no game knowledge can misrepresent their bluffs or give themselves up simply because they don't know how players typically act in the role they're bluffing.

## 3. Deception
The ability to lie convincingly — but critically, **this applies to good players too.** Knowing when and how to lie while good is critical to becoming stronger.

Key insight: Establishing a baseline tendency to lie early regardless of alignment gives flexibility in evil games. Players who always tell the truth when good will struggle to act the same way when evil — savvy social players pick up on cross-game patterns.

Good-side deception example: The Saint openly claiming on day one removes all their options. If the Saint can convince the demon they're worth killing, it's huge upside for town.

## 4. Persuasion
The ability to convince the town of your perspective. No matter how well one can solve the game, failing to win others to your side results in defeat.

Key insight: **The most underrated axis.** The loudest player can command the town more effectively than the smartest player. "I called it" means nothing if the game ended in an evil win because you couldn't get the vote.

Even quiet players can persuade effectively — making the right points to the right players in private can match commanding the floor publicly.

## 5. Social Insight
Solving through social reads rather than logic. Highest variance skill — a correct read can solve a coin-flip game, but tunnel vision on a misplaced read can pit two good players against each other.

Most viable and valuable when playing with a consistent group. Dangerous to make assumptions about strangers, especially skilled veterans.

Evil application: knowing who good players are suspicious of, to set up framing toward the end. Failure to do this can convict the demon by implicit elimination even when the logic says it's a coin flip.
